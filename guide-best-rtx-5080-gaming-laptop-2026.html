Why TGP Is Everything With RTX 5080 Laptops

Every laptop on this list uses the Nvidia GeForce RTX 5080 Laptop GPU — the same silicon. But the performance difference between the best and worst machine here is over 25%. The reason: Total Graphics Power (TGP). Nvidia allows laptop manufacturers to configure RTX 5080 anywhere from 80W to 175W. At 80W, it performs like a desktop RTX 4070. At 175W, it performs like a desktop RTX 4090.

Always check TGP before buying. A machine advertised as "RTX 5080" at $1,499 is almost certainly running at a throttled 80–100W. The laptops on this list all run at 120W minimum, and the top picks at 175W.

Should You Buy RTX 5080 or Wait for RTX 5090?

The RTX 5090 laptop GPU exists but commands a $1,000–$1,500 premium over RTX 5080 configurations. In our testing, the RTX 5090 delivers roughly 20–25% more raster performance at the same TGP. That's a meaningful gap, but at the price difference — you're looking at $3,500+ vs $2,000 for the Legion — it's hard to justify unless you specifically need 4K native gaming or extreme ray tracing fidelity.

For 1440p gaming with DLSS 4 enabled, the RTX 5080 at 175W is already delivering 120+ fps in demanding titles. The RTX 5090 gets you to 140–150fps at the same settings. You're paying $1,000+ for roughly 20 extra frames per second that DLSS 4 Multi Frame Generation can synthesize anyway.

Our Recommendation Buy the Lenovo Legion Pro 7i Gen 10 at $1,999. It's the best-performing RTX 5080 laptop for gaming, has the best display, and leaves $500–$1,500 in your pocket vs the 5090 alternatives. Spend that money on peripherals, a monitor, or save it.
